# AI Humanizer — De-AI-ifying Generated Text

A skill that detects and removes the stylistic tells of AI-generated
prose. Used by the `/humanizer` command and by any project that ships
text written by an LLM but read by humans. The premise: AI-style prose
is recognizable, and recognition reduces the perceived authority of the
content. De-AI-ifying does NOT change the meaning; it changes the voice.

## When to invoke

- The user invokes `/humanizer <text>`
- A blog post, README, doc page, or marketing copy is generated by the agent
- A long-form output is about to be shown to an external audience
- The output will be archived, indexed, or syndicated

## When NOT to invoke

- Code, technical documentation, API references — keep them precise even at the cost of voice
- Internal notes the user reads alone
- Output that is intentionally formal (legal text, contracts)

## The AI Tells (and how to remove them)

### Tell 1 — Em-dash overuse

AI prose loves the em-dash for compound clauses. A human writer uses
em-dashes sparingly; AI uses them in almost every paragraph.

Detection: more than 1 em-dash per 200 words.

Removal:
- Replace with a comma + relative clause when natural
- Replace with two short sentences when the clause is independent
- Keep the em-dash for one or two genuinely emphatic uses per piece

Example:
- AI: "The plan — which had been carefully designed — was abandoned."
- Human: "The plan, carefully designed, was abandoned." (or two sentences)

### Tell 2 — Hedging phrases

AI hedges constantly: "it's worth noting that...", "one might argue...",
"in some cases...". Humans state things directly when they're confident.

Detection: phrases like:
- "It's worth noting that"
- "It is important to note"
- "Generally speaking"
- "In many cases"
- "Some might argue"
- "Various"
- "Numerous"
- "A variety of"

Removal: just delete the hedge. The remaining sentence is usually stronger.

Example:
- AI: "It's worth noting that the function generally returns null in some cases."
- Human: "The function returns null when the user is not found."

### Tell 3 — Passive voice without reason

AI defaults to passive voice. Humans use active voice unless the actor is
unknown or unimportant.

Detection: regex for `was/were/is/are <past-participle> by`.

Removal: rewrite in active voice unless the actor is genuinely unknown.

Example:
- AI: "The bug was fixed by the engineer."
- Human: "The engineer fixed the bug."

### Tell 4 — Tricolons everywhere

AI loves three-item lists: "fast, reliable, and scalable", "concise, clear,
and complete". Humans use them occasionally, not paragraph after paragraph.

Detection: more than 1 three-item list per 200 words.

Removal: collapse to one or two terms; use a single sentence if the list
is decorative rather than informative.

### Tell 5 — Generic transitions

AI uses transition words as filler:
- "Moreover"
- "Furthermore"
- "Additionally"
- "In conclusion"
- "Ultimately"
- "Overall"
- "In summary"

Detection: any of these words at sentence-start.

Removal: just delete. The sentence stands on its own.

### Tell 6 — Adverb stacking

AI stacks adverbs: "extremely effectively", "remarkably efficiently",
"truly exceptional". Humans use one good adverb or none.

Detection: adverb count per 100 words > 5.

Removal: keep the strongest adverb in each sentence; drop the rest.

### Tell 7 — Generic praise

AI loves to call things "powerful", "robust", "comprehensive", "elegant",
"sophisticated". Humans describe what something DOES.

Detection: bigram frequency of `<adjective> <noun>` from the AI-praise list.

Removal: replace with a concrete description.

Example:
- AI: "A robust and comprehensive solution."
- Human: "It handles 50,000 requests per second and recovers from disk failures within 200ms."

### Tell 8 — "It's not just X, it's Y"

AI loves the contrast pattern: "it's not just fast — it's blazingly fast",
"this isn't merely a tool, it's a platform".

Detection: regex for `not just <X> [,—] it's <Y>` or `not merely <X> <Y>`.

Removal: rewrite as a direct statement of what it is.

## Process

### Step 1 — Scan

Run all 8 detection passes over the input text. Tag matches with their
location and tell-type.

### Step 2 — Prioritize

Sort matches by impact:
- High: passive voice in main clauses, tricolon paragraphs, "not just X"
- Medium: em-dash overuse, hedging
- Low: transitions, adverb stacking, generic praise

### Step 3 — Edit

Apply edits in order. After each edit, re-read the surrounding sentences
to ensure the meaning is preserved.

### Step 4 — Verify

Run the detection passes again. The text should have:
- ≤ 1 em-dash per 200 words
- ≤ 1 tricolon per 200 words
- 0 leading "Moreover/Furthermore/Additionally"
- 0 hedging phrases from the canonical list
- Active voice in all main clauses (passive only when actor is unknown)

If the text still trips a detection, edit again.

## Examples

### Example — before and after

AI version:
> "It's worth noting that our solution is not just fast — it's remarkably
> fast. Moreover, it's been carefully engineered by our team to be robust,
> reliable, and scalable. Generally speaking, the system handles a variety
> of workloads, and it's been designed with performance in mind."

Humanized:
> "The system is fast. We engineered it to handle 50,000 requests per
> second and recover from disk failures within 200ms."

The humanized version is shorter, more concrete, and trips zero of the 8
detection passes.

## Anti-patterns

- Over-de-AI-ifying technical docs where precision matters
- Removing the em-dash count to zero (one or two are fine — humans use them too)
- Replacing every passive with active without checking whether the actor is actually known
- Deleting all transitions (some are useful — just not as filler)
- Going beyond style: changing the actual claims of the text

## Stopping criteria

- Detection scores are within human-writing thresholds
- Meaning is preserved
- Voice is consistent throughout
- Length is shorter than the original (de-AI-ifying almost always tightens)
